Magpupungko Rock Pools: A Tide-Timing Guide

By the suroyIAO team Updated June 2026 Nature 8 min read

Magpupungko is the postcard you've seen: a vivid turquoise pool ringed by dark rock, with a famous boulder perched improbably on top. What the postcard doesn't tell you is that the pool is only there for part of the day. Show up at the wrong tide and you'll find the sea has swallowed the whole thing. Timing isn't a detail here — it's the entire visit.

Why the tide is everything

The rock pools at Magpupungko are carved into a tidal flat on Siargao's northeast coast. At high tide the ocean covers the rocks completely, and there are no pools to speak of — just sea. As the tide drops, the water retreats and leaves behind the natural pools, clear and swimmable, framed by the exposed rock formations. So your visit lives or dies by one question: when is low tide today?

The one ruleGo on a falling or low tide. Aim to arrive ahead of low tide so you get the longest, calmest window in the pools. Arriving at high tide means a long drive for nothing.

How to time it (the part most guides skip)

Tide times shift by roughly an hour each day and follow a cycle, so "yesterday it was good at 10am" tells you almost nothing about today. Don't rely on a generic memory or a friend's old photo. Instead:

  1. Check the actual tide for the day you plan to go. You want a low-tide window that falls during daylight and ideally late-morning to afternoon, so you're not racing the dark.
  2. Plan to arrive an hour or so before the low. This gives you the pools at their best and the most time before the water returns.
  3. Factor the drive. Magpupungko is up the coast from General Luna — a real ride, not a quick hop. Work backwards from the tide window to your departure time.

Getting there

Magpupungko sits in the Pilar area, well north of the General Luna hub. Most visitors ride a scooter up or join a north-island day tour that bundles it with other stops. The roads are scenic but require attention — the usual Siargao mix of sand, dogs and the occasional rough patch. There's a small entrance/environmental fee collected locally, and local caretakers are often around; respecting their guidance keeps the spot pleasant for everyone.

What to bring

  • Reef-safe sunscreen. There's limited shade on the rock flat, and the marine environment deserves the protection.
  • Water shoes or sandals with grip. The rocks can be sharp and slippery — bare feet are a quick way to a cut.
  • Cash for the entrance fee and any local refreshments.
  • A dry bag for your phone, especially if you want photos from the water.
  • Drinking water. It gets hot out on the exposed rock.

Safety on the rocks

The famous jump from the boulder looks irresistible, but water depth changes with the tide and conditions, and rock pools are not bottomless. Never jump without confirming it's deep enough right now — ask the local caretakers, who know the spot far better than any blog. Watch your footing on wet rock, keep an eye on children, and respect any signs or instructions. The pools are beautiful precisely because they're wild; treat them with the caution wild places deserve.

Make a day of it

Because the drive north is significant, it's worth pairing Magpupungko with other northern stops rather than treating it as a solo errand. The north of the island holds quiet beaches, riverside hangouts and cliffside eateries that reward the journey. Time your Magpupungko window first — since it's the one fixed point dictated by the tide — then build the rest of the day's loop around it.

The bottom line

Magpupungko is genuinely one of Siargao's most striking natural sights, and it's almost entirely free if you get the timing right. The visitors who come away disappointed are nearly always the ones who didn't check the tide. Do that one thing — confirm a daytime low tide and plan around it — and you'll get the turquoise pools, the dramatic rocks, and the swim that made the place famous.
